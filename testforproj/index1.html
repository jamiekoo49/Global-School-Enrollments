Global School Enrollment and Its Impacts on Socioeconomic Factors

Sahana Dhar, Madelyn Redick, Sophia Watanabe, Jamie Koo

Introduction

Global school enrollment is a critical indicator of educational access and quality worldwide, impacting individual and societal development in numerous ways. Higher enrollment rates are often associated with a more educated workforce, which can drive innovation, enhance productivity, and contribute to economic growth. Additionally, analyzing global school enrollment data can provide insights into future economic prospects for different countries and regions. In another aspect, education is linked to a wide range of societal and health benefits, including mortality rates. Exploring the impacts of school enrollment can highlight the importance of education in achieving broader societal goals. Also, data on global school enrollment can inform policymakers, international organizations, and investors about areas where educational infrastructure and support are most needed. This can help direct resources more effectively, ensuring that investments in education yield the greatest societal and economic returns.

Our dataset on global education is sourced from The World Bank, and specifically focuses on school enrollment. The dataset is a comprehensive collection of data that spans multiple countries and covers various years. With 43,629 rows and 67 columns of data, this dataset offers an in-depth look at educational indicators, including enrollment rates across different education levels (primary, secondary, and tertiary) and other relevant metrics such as progression to secondary education by gender. This dataset enables a wide range of analyses from trend analysis and comparative studies across regions or countries to evaluate the impact of educational policies. Furthermore, it allows for exploring the connection between education and socioeconomic factors like economic growth, health outcomes, and gender equality.

Interactive Plots

This interactive scatterplot has primary school enrollment levels on the x axis and a dynamic y axis with options to plot unemployment rates, literacy rates, progression to secondary school, and more. It features an interactive legend that shows only data from a selected continent. Hovering over a specific data point shows the name of the country and the year in which the data was collected. This plot is extremely helpful in understanding the dataset as a whole, since it allows us to compare how global enrollment rates impacts other variables of interest. For example, when ‘Progression to secondary school (%)’ is selected as the y axis variable, there is a dense cluster of regions with high primary school enrollment rates and high levels of progression from primary school to secondary school, while other portions of the data are more widespread. This indicates that there is a strong correlation between the two variables, that there is some type of relationship between them. However, the presence of outliers and variations across regions suggests that there are factors other than primary school enrollment involved in progression rates.

Map

Data on compulsory education duration becomes available starting from 1995, with North Korea being the sole country with available data for that year, mandating 11 years of compulsory education. By 1998, data becomes available for many countries. The maximum compulsory education duration observed across countries in any given year is 17 years (Venezuela in 2021), while the minimum is 5 years for several nations. Overall, while many countries were steadfast in the number of compulsory years, others increased the number of years of compulsory education. The best example of this is South America. Many countries in this region have increased the number of years over time from below 10 to over 10 over the course of two decades. In 2022 (the most recent year with available data), it's evident that South America overall has the highest number of compulsory years of education. This contrasts with Africa, which varies more by country, but generally requires fewer years of compulsory education on average. Other regions, such as North America, tend to be more inconsistent. It's important to remember that the years of compulsory education are not equivalent to actual school enrollment, nor do they equate to educational quality. Rather, this map seeks to provide a baseline understanding of the amount of education mandated by governments worldwide and to what degree this varies from country to country.
